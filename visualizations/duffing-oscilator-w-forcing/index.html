Forced Duffing Oscillator

When a nonlinear spring meets periodic driving, chaos is never far behind.

Driving a Nonlinear Oscillator

The Duffing oscillator augments the familiar mass-spring model with a nonlinear restoring force. When we introduce a periodic driving term on top of that nonlinearity, the system can wander between orderly, periodic motion and wildly chaotic trajectories. The equation of motion is

$$ \ddot{x} + \delta \dot{x} + \beta x + \alpha x^3 = F_0 \cos(\omega t) $$

where \(x\) is displacement, \(\delta\) controls damping, \(\beta\) and \(\alpha\) set the shape of the double-well potential, and the forcing amplitude \(F_0\) and frequency \(\omega\) continually pump energy into the oscillator. Competing influences between damping, nonlinearity, and driving energy make the phase portrait especially rich.

Parameters You Can Tune

  • Damping (\(\delta\)): Larger values bleed energy faster, calming chaotic swings; low damping allows long-lived transients.
  • Linear stiffness (\(\beta\)): Shapes the quadratic part of the potential. Negative values create two wells separated by an unstable equilibrium.
  • Nonlinear stiffness (\(\alpha\)): Controls how steep the wells become away from the origin. Increasing \(\alpha\) accentuates the double-well behavior.
  • Forcing amplitude (\(F_0\)): Determines how hard the system is driven. Even modest increases can trigger period doubling cascades.
  • Forcing frequency (\(\omega\)): Sets how quickly the driver oscillates. Matching natural frequencies can lead to resonance, while mismatches create intricate beats.
  • View range: Adjust the window on phase space to zoom in on local dynamics or pan back to catch global structure.

How to Use the Explorer

Click anywhere in the phase portrait to launch a trajectory from that initial condition. The simulator integrates forward in time while simultaneously drawing the vector field (when enabled) so you can interpret the flow. Toggle the vector field to reveal the instantaneous direction of motion, and experiment with adding multiple trajectories to compare their long-term fates under the same parameters.

Things to Try

  • Start with light damping and increase the forcing amplitude to watch the system transition from a periodic orbit to a chaotic attractor.
  • Fix \(F_0\) and vary the driving frequency to see bands of resonance appear and disappear.
  • Contrast runs launched from the left and right wells; small parameter tweaks often decide which basin of attraction wins.
