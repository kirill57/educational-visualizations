The Duffing Oscillator

An Archetype of Chaos

The Duffing Oscillator is a non-linear second-order differential equation used to model certain damped and driven oscillators. It is a classic example of a system that exhibits chaotic behavior, where the long-term evolution is highly sensitive to the initial conditions. This "sensitive dependence" is a hallmark of chaos, often visualized by the intricate, fractal structures the system traces in its phase space.

The Governing Equations

The dynamics are described by a system of two first-order equations, where \(x\) represents position and \(y\) represents velocity:

$$ \frac{dx}{dt} = y $$ $$ \frac{dy}{dt} = x - x^3 - \delta y $$

The term \(x - x^3\) creates a "double-well" potential, meaning the system has two stable resting points. The term \(-\delta y\) represents damping, which removes energy from the system. The interplay between these forces can lead to complex and unpredictable motion.

Exploring the Parameters

In this visualization, you can adjust the damping parameter \(\delta\) (delta) and click to introduce new trajectories.

  • Low Damping: With small values of \(\delta\), trajectories can be quite complex and take a long time to settle into one of the attractors.
  • High Damping: As you increase \(\delta\), the system loses energy more quickly, and trajectories will more rapidly spiral into one of the two stable points at \((\pm 1, 0)\).

Even without an external driving force (which is often included in the Duffing equation), the system's phase portrait reveals the boundaries (basins of attraction) between the two stable states. Trajectories starting on one side of a complex dividing line will end up in the left well, while those on the other side will end up in the right well.
