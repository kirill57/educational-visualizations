The Harmonic Oscillator

From simple vibrations to resonance, the harmonic oscillator sits at the heart of mathematical physics.

Why This System Matters

The harmonic oscillator is the workhorse of differential equations. Whether you are modeling a mass on a spring, the small-angle motion of a pendulum, current in an LC circuit, or even vibrations of molecules, you start with the same core idea: a restoring force that pulls the system back toward equilibrium and is proportional to the displacement.

In the linear regime this restoring force produces sinusoidal motion that never grows or decays. Add a little friction and the motion fades away. Add the right kind of periodic input and the response can build dramatically. All of those cases live inside the visualization below.

The Governing Equation

With mass scaled to one, the damped and driven harmonic oscillator satisfies

$$\frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} + 2\zeta\,\omega_0\,\frac{dx}{dt} + \omega_0^2 x = F\cos(\Omega t).$$

The parameters mean:

  • \(\omega_0\) is the natural frequency. It sets the pace the system would oscillate without damping or forcing.
  • \(\zeta\) is the dimensionless damping ratio, which defines the system's behavior:
    • Underdamped (\(0 < \zeta < 1\)): The system oscillates, but the amplitude decays over time.
    • Critically Damped (\(\zeta = 1\)): The system returns to equilibrium as fast as possible without any oscillation.
    • Overdamped (\(\zeta > 1\)): The system returns to equilibrium slowly and without oscillating.
  • \(F\) and \(\Omega\) control the amplitude and frequency of an external periodic drive.

What to Explore

Use the controls in the simulation to investigate classic behaviours:

  • Set the forcing to None and vary the damping ratio to see the transition from persistent oscillations to heavy decay.
  • Switch on the cosine drive, then sweep the drive frequency. Notice how the amplitude peaks when \(\Omega\) approaches \(\omega_0\): this is resonance.
  • Start with a large initial displacement and introduce gentle damping to watch energy trade between kinetic and potential forms over time.

These ideas extend far beyond a spring and mass. Quantum oscillators, atoms in a lattice, and even the modes of a drumhead reduce to this same equation with different interpretations of the parameters.
