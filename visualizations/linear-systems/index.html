2x2 Linear Systems

Understanding phase portraits through eigenvalues, trace, and determinant

Why Linear Systems Matter

Linear systems of ordinary differential equations form the foundation of dynamical systems theory. They appear everywhere: in mechanical vibrations, electrical circuits, population dynamics, and as the linearization of more complex nonlinear systems near equilibrium points. The beauty of linear systems lies in their complete classification—every 2×2 linear system falls into one of eight distinct categories based solely on its eigenvalues.

The phase portrait—a visual representation of all possible trajectories—reveals the system's long-term behavior at a glance. Whether trajectories spiral inward, diverge outward, or form closed loops depends entirely on the eigenvalues of the system's coefficient matrix.

The Governing Equations

A 2×2 linear system of ODEs has the form:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$$

where \(A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\) is a constant matrix. The behavior of this system is determined by the eigenvalues \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) of \(A\), which satisfy the characteristic equation:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 - T\lambda + D = 0$$

where \(T = a + d\) is the trace and \(D = ad - bc\) is the determinant of \(A\). The discriminant \(\Delta = T^2 - 4D\) determines whether the eigenvalues are real or complex.

Classification of Phase Portraits

The eight distinct types of phase portraits are organized by the nature of the eigenvalues:

Stable Node

Eigenvalues: Both real, negative, and distinct

Behavior: Curved paths converging to the origin. All trajectories approach equilibrium exponentially.

Unstable Node

Eigenvalues: Both real, positive, and distinct

Behavior: Curved paths diverging from the origin. All trajectories move away from equilibrium.

Saddle

Eigenvalues: Real with opposite signs

Behavior: Hyperbolic paths with one stable direction (eigenvector for negative eigenvalue) and one unstable direction (eigenvector for positive eigenvalue).

Stable Spiral

Eigenvalues: Complex conjugate pair with negative real part

Behavior: Trajectories spiral inward toward the origin, oscillating as they approach equilibrium.

Unstable Spiral

Eigenvalues: Complex conjugate pair with positive real part

Behavior: Trajectories spiral outward from the origin, oscillating as they diverge from equilibrium.

Center

Eigenvalues: Purely imaginary (zero real part)

Behavior: Closed elliptical orbits around the origin. The system is conservative with no energy dissipation.

Stable Proper Node (Star)

Eigenvalues: Real, negative, and equal with two independent eigenvectors

Behavior: Straight radial paths converging to the origin. All trajectories approach along straight lines.

Stable Improper Node (Degenerate)

Eigenvalues: Real, negative, and equal with only one independent eigenvector

Behavior: Parabolic paths converging to the origin with shearing. Trajectories curve as they approach equilibrium.

The Trace-Determinant Diagram

The visualization above includes a trace-determinant diagram that maps every possible 2×2 linear system onto a plane. The horizontal axis represents the trace \(T\), and the vertical axis represents the determinant \(D\). The parabola \(D = T^2/4\) separates real eigenvalues (below) from complex eigenvalues (above). This diagram provides a complete classification:

  • Below the parabola (\(D < T^2/4\)): Real eigenvalues. The sign of \(T\) determines stability (negative = stable, positive = unstable).
  • Above the parabola (\(D > T^2/4\)): Complex eigenvalues. The sign of \(T\) determines whether spirals are stable (negative) or unstable (positive).
  • On the parabola (\(D = T^2/4\)): Repeated eigenvalues, leading to proper or improper nodes.
  • Below the T-axis (\(D < 0\)): Saddle points, with one positive and one negative eigenvalue.

What to Explore

This classification extends naturally to higher-dimensional linear systems, where the eigenvalues continue to determine stability and the qualitative nature of solutions. Understanding these eight cases provides the foundation for analyzing more complex nonlinear systems through linearization.
