Simple Harmonic Oscillator

Pure sinusoidal motion with no damping and no driving - just the restoring force doing its work.

The Minimal Model

Strip a mass-spring system down to its essentials, and you get the simple harmonic oscillator. With no friction to sap energy and no external forcing, the equation of motion reduces to a perfectly balanced exchange between kinetic and potential energy. The resulting motion is sinusoidal, with a frequency set entirely by the spring constant.

The Governing Equation

Assuming a unit mass, the motion satisfies

\(\frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} + kx = 0.\)

The parameter \(k\) is the spring constant. Its square root determines the natural angular frequency \(\omega_0 = \sqrt{k}\) and the period \(T = 2\pi/\omega_0\). The total mechanical energy is conserved:

\(E = \tfrac{1}{2} k x^2 + \tfrac{1}{2} v^2.\)

Reading the Live Meters

The dashboard in the interactive keeps a running tally of the system state so you can connect the plots to the underlying mechanics:

  • t is the simulation time in seconds. It advances only while the animation is running.
  • x measures the instantaneous displacement of the mass from equilibrium (metres).
  • v is the velocity (metres per second), the rate at which the mass crosses the midpoint.
  • E is the total mechanical energy. With no damping it should remain constant; any change hints at numerical round-off.
  • \(\omega_0\) is the natural angular frequency dictated by the current spring constant, \(\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}\).
  • T is the period of one complete oscillation, \(T = 2\pi/\omega_0\).

What to Try

  • Set an initial displacement with zero velocity and watch the phase portrait trace a perfect horizontal ellipse.
  • Give the mass an initial push instead, leaving the displacement at zero. The ellipse rotates to align with the velocity axis.
  • Adjust the spring constant and notice how the ellipse squeezes in the velocity direction while the period decreases.
  • Choose mismatched initial position and velocity to plot a generic ellipse whose area reports the conserved energy.

These same phase-space ellipses appear in everything from small-angle pendulums to electromagnetic oscillators, making the simple harmonic oscillator an indispensable baseline model.
