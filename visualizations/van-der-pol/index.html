The Van der Pol Oscillator

A Self-Sustaining Oscillation

The Van der Pol oscillator is a classic example of a non-conservative oscillator with non-linear damping. Developed by Dutch electrical engineer Balthasar van der Pol in the 1920s, it was originally used to model vacuum tube circuits. Unlike a simple pendulum, which loses energy and stops, the Van der Pol system can sustain its own oscillations indefinitely by drawing energy from a power source, a behavior known as a limit cycle.

The Governing Equations

The system is described by a second-order differential equation, which can be written as a system of two first-order equations. The variables are \(x\) (position) and \(y\) (velocity).

$$ \frac{dx}{dt} = y $$ $$ \frac{dy}{dt} = \mu(1-x^2)y - x $$

The Role of Î¼ (mu)

The parameter \(\mu\) is a non-negative constant that controls the strength of the non-linear damping.

  • When \(\mu = 0\), the system becomes a simple harmonic oscillator, and the trajectories are perfect circles or ellipses.
  • When \(\mu > 0\), the system exhibits a limit cycle. If the system starts near the origin (small oscillations), the damping term is negative, adding energy and causing the amplitude to grow. If the system starts far from the origin (large oscillations), the damping is positive, removing energy and causing the amplitude to shrink.

The Limit Cycle

The most fascinating feature of the Van der Pol oscillator is its limit cycle. This is a closed trajectory in the phase space. Regardless of the initial conditions (as long as they are not exactly at the unstable origin), the system's trajectory will always converge to this specific loop. This makes the oscillator incredibly stable and predictable, which is why it's a valuable model for many phenomena in physics, electronics, biology (like heartbeats), and other fields.
